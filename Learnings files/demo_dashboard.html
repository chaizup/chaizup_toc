What is TOC?

Theory of Constraints (TOC) is a management philosophy by Dr. Eliyahu Goldratt. The big idea: every system has ONE single bottleneck that limits total output. Fix that one thing first. At Chaizup, the bottleneck is the VFFS (Vertical Form Fill Seal) pouch-filling machine.

🔩 VFFS = Vertical Form Fill Seal — fills and seals premix tea pouches at ~35/min

Why Buffer Management?

Traditional FMCG companies use forecasts — wrong 30–40% of the time. This creates the bimodal trap: stocked out on fast movers, excess on slow movers. TOC replaces forecasting with Buffer Management — replenish exactly what was consumed today.

✅ 30–45% less inventory · 97%+ fill rate · Zero forecast required

Target Buffer Sizing

How big should the buffer be? This foundational formula determines the maximum stock to hold for each SKU or material.

Concept
A buffer is a planned safety cushion of stock. It protects Chaizup from two types of variability: demand variability (customers buy more or less than expected) and supply variability (supplier delivers late or short). The larger the variability, the larger the buffer needed.
Target Buffer = ADU × RLT × VF

Formula 2

Inventory Position (IP)

Your TRUE stock level — not just what is on the shelf, but everything that counts toward future supply.

Concept — FG Inventory Position
On-hand stock alone is misleading. WIP (work in progress — product on the VFFS line) will be available very soon, so it counts. Backorders (already promised to distributors) are spoken for and must be deducted. True IP tells you exactly how much demand you can actually fill.
IP (FG) = On-Hand + WIP − Backorders

Formula 3

Buffer Penetration % & Stock Remaining %

THE most important number in the system. Two ways to measure the same thing — both correct, opposite directions.

Key Insight — Two Methods, Same Conclusion
Method A (SR%) asks "How FULL is the buffer?" — High number = safe. Low number = danger.
Method B (BP%) asks "How EMPTY is the buffer?" — Low number = safe. High number = danger.

They always add up to 100%. SR% + BP% = 100% (always, without exception).
BP% = (Target − IP) ÷ Target × 100    |    SR% = IP ÷ Target × 100    |    BP% + SR% = 100%

Formula 4

Replenishment Quantity

How much to produce or order? The answer: exactly enough to restore the buffer to its target. No more, no less.

Concept — Pull System
TOC uses a PULL system — you only replenish what was actually consumed. This is the opposite of traditional MRP which uses a PUSH system (forecast next month, produce in advance). By ordering exactly Target − IP, Chaizup avoids both overstocking and understocking. The buffer then naturally refills to 100%.
Order Quantity = Target Buffer − Inventory Position

Formula 5

Throughput per Constraint Unit (T/CU)

The tie-breaker. When two SKUs have the same BP%, which earns Chaizup more money per minute of VFFS time?

Concept
Throughput (T) = Selling Price minus ONLY truly variable costs (RM + PM). Labour, electricity, rent, and machine depreciation are NOT deducted — they are fixed costs that don't change regardless of which SKU you produce. They cannot affect the relative priority of one SKU over another.

T/CU = Rupees earned per minute of VFFS (bottleneck) time. Higher T/CU = runs first when BP% is tied.
T = Price − RM Cost − PM Cost    |    T/CU = T ÷ Constraint Minutes per Unit

Dynamic Buffer Management

DBM — Self-Correcting Buffers

Buffers set on day one will eventually become wrong. DBM automatically adjusts them based on real behaviour — no forecasts.

🔴 Too Much Red (TMR)
If stock stays in the Red zone for more than 20% of the RLT in a single replenishment cycle, the buffer is too small. Stockouts are happening or near. Increase buffer by 33%.
New Buffer = Current Buffer × 1.33
🟢 Too Much Green (TMG)
If stock stays in the Green zone for 3 complete replenishment cycles, the buffer is too large. Cash is locked up unnecessarily. Decrease buffer by 33%.
New Buffer = Current Buffer × 0.67
🧠 DBM Asymmetry Explained

DBM is deliberately asymmetric. Increasing the buffer triggers after just 20% of RLT in Red (fast), but decreasing the buffer requires 3 full cycles in Green (slow).

This is intentional. The cost of a stockout — lost sales, angry distributors, emergency air freight, production downtime — almost always exceeds the cost of holding a little extra inventory. TOC protects the constraint.

Demand Adjustment Factor

DAF — Seasonal Buffer Adjustment

For predictable events like Diwali, summer slowdowns, and trade promotions — temporarily adjust buffers without touching DBM.

Concept
DBM adjusts buffers based on past actual behaviour. DAF adjusts buffers based on known future events. Set by the Supply Chain Manager at the monthly S&OP meeting. Applied temporarily for the event window, then reverted to the baseline buffer.
Adjusted Buffer = Current Target Buffer × DAF
